When the Leaves Stop Shaking

Last updated on July 24, 2022

I spent a happy year chasing shaky leaves and building the first draft of my family tree. I learned so much, collecting census records and Find A Grave memorials and a reference or two to military service. I dutifully downloaded all the documents just like the “getting started” blogs recommended, and I painstakingly typed everything into my genealogy software: date and place of birth, date and place of death, parents’ and children’s names, where they were living during each of the census enumerations. I was quite pleased with the charts I was producing.

And then I started noticing that I had some problems.

Sometimes things in my tree didn’t make a whole lot of sense or were downright impossible. Was John really likely to have died in St. Lawrence county in 1841 if only a year earlier he had been enumerated in Schoharie county at the other end of the state? How could Nancy’s daughter have been born in 1888 if she herself wasn’t born until 1890?

I also was getting to a point on every single line where the leaves just stopped shaking. I had traced every hint, read every offered document, and still I couldn’t find the answers I needed to move forward. No record, it seemed, was going to be able to tell me who Harriet’s parents were.

I had built myself some pretty solid brick walls.

But by then I was hooked. I did the only thing I knew to do - I went back to reading back to reading. Fortunately, the internet is full of advice for the blossoming genealogist. Pretty soon, I had a pretty good idea what I should do to get past my brick walls:

  • define a research question
  • use good techniques to perform reasonably exhaustive research
  • build and use a locality guide
  • analyze sources
  • write up my results

It all seemed pretty straightforward. The trouble was, although lots of blogs could tell me WHAT I needed to do, very few books or blogs were very clear about HOW to do it.

I’ve figured out a lot of secrets that work for me. Some of them go directly against professional advice. I will share them here, but I also encourage you to do your own research, see what the experts say, and experiment to see what works for you.

The first thing I did to get past my addiction to shaky leaves was that I needed to broaden my thinking.
